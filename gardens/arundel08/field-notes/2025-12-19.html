Waterways & Swales

Water is one of the quiet architects of an ecological garden. By shaping the land with gentle swales, contour-led channels, and slow-moving waterways, rainfall is captured rather than lost. These forms hold moisture in the soil, reduce runoff, and create pockets of microclimate where plants thrive with less intervention. Swales also guide the eye and the body through the landscape, becoming subtle pathways of movement and seasonal change. This approach reconnects the garden to its natural hydrology, restoring patterns that once shaped local ecologies.

Habitat Rocks & Logs

Habitat elements such as rocks and fallen logs provide the structure, complexity, and shelter that wildlife depends on. Strategically placed boulders create thermal mass for lizards, insects, and soil life, while hollow logs and decaying timber offer refuge for skinks, microbats, beetles, and fungi. These materials anchor the garden in place and add a timeless, grounded presence to the design. More than aesthetic accents, they operate as miniature ecosystems -- stabilising soil, supporting moisture retention, and forming the essential building blocks for biodiversity.

Canopy Layer

The canopy layer is the long-term architecture of the garden -- the tallest trees that anchor the design, shape the microclimate, and link the property to the broader green corridor of South Surrey Park. At Arundel, the canopy comprises 3% of the planting density, with approximately 32 canopy trees distributed carefully across the 475m2 space. Their spacing allows them to mature without crowding while still creating an interconnected canopy that supports bird movement and enhances biodiversity. These trees form the garden's upper frame -- a gesture of reciprocity to the parkland beyond the fence and a long-term ecological investment that will define the garden for decades.

Sub-Canopy Layer

The sub-canopy layer is the woodland heart of the garden -- the 3-6 metre trees and large shrubs that provide dappled shade, structure, and vertical variation. This layer regulates temperature, slows wind, enriches soil through leaf litter, and forms a protective envelope under which the ground layer thrives. At Arundel, this layer represents 4% of the total planting, adding approximately 42 sub-canopy trees across the rear garden. Spaced to create alternating pools of light and shade, they ensure the dense Miyawaki-inspired underplanting can establish vigorously. Their placement reinforces the natural topography -- gathering around swales, stretching along slope lines, and framing the internal forest rooms.

Shrub Layer

The shrub layer provides the garden with structure, rhythm, and habitat complexity. These mid-storey species introduce seasonal colour, flowering pulses, density for small birds, and layered textures that soften the transitions between ground and canopy. Ecologically, shrubs offer shelter, nesting opportunities, and nectar sources that support insects and birds throughout the year. At Arundel, this layer accounts for 25% of the planting density, contributing around 262 shrubs across the site. Placed in naturalistic clusters, they define movement corridors, frame views toward South Surrey Park, and create pockets of enclosure along the meandering paths.

Ground Layer

The ground layer is the living fabric of the garden -- the continuous weave of grasses, sedges, herbs, and forbs that drives most of the ecological function. This layer stabilises soil, cools the microclimate, suppresses weeds, and supports insect life at an extraordinary scale. At Arundel, this layer forms the foundation of the forested rear garden. With an ecological density of 3 plants per m2, the ground layer contributes approximately 714 plants across the site -- a thick, species-rich understorey that quickly knits the soil surface together. These plants occupy every microclimate shaped by the site's contours: sunny rises, cooler swales, and moisture-holding depressions. In ecological design, the ground layer is not filler -- it is the engine room.
