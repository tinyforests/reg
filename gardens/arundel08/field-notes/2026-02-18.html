Burrowing frog sighting -- lower slope refuge zone

Burrowing frog (Neobatrachus sp.) confirmed in the lower slope refuge zone, three days after habitat log installation. Single specimen observed moving between the embedded log structure and the soil edge at the moisture zone boundary. Sighting confirmed during a site visit and documented with photographic evidence. This is the first verified fauna record for Arundel and represents a significant ecological signal -- burrowing frogs are sensitive indicators of soil permeability, moisture retention, and habitat connectivity.

Why this matters

  • -- Burrowing frogs are sensitive ecological indicators. Their presence confirms that soil permeability, moisture retention, and habitat structure are functioning at a level that supports amphibian life.
  • -- Three days after log installation is a remarkable response time -- the frog was almost certainly already present in the broader area, likely using the adjacent corridor connecting to Surrey Hills Reserve 80 metres north.
  • -- This sighting is the first verified fauna record for the Surrey Hills corridor cluster and adds direct evidence to the corridor node claim.
  • -- Amphibian presence in urban gardens is rare. It requires the right combination of moisture, permeability, refuge, and chemical-free management. Arundel has all four.

Ecological significance

The speed of this response -- three days -- is not coincidental. Surrey Hills Reserve 80 metres to the north provides the reservoir population. The ecological corridor between the reserve and this garden, though informal and fragmented, was permeable enough for a single individual to move through and locate the new habitat structure. This is the network in action. As more gardens along this corridor register and improve, the movement corridor becomes more legible, more reliable, and more measurable.
